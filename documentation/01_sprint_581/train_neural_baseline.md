## Overview
This document outlines the evaluation of our neural baseline. A fine-tuned **DistilBERT** (`distilbert-base-uncased`) model was trained for 3-class email classification (Ham / Phish / Spam) using class-weighted cross-entropy loss, linear learning-rate warmup, and early stopping on validation macro-F1.

The model was trained on **105 Gold-annotated samples**, validated on **12 samples**, and tested on a strictly isolated test set of **16 samples**. All splits were generated by `split.py` with seed 581 and stratified by label.

---

## 1. Quantitative Results (The Numbers)

The neural baseline achieved a **68.75% Overall Accuracy** and a **Macro-F1 of 0.5444** on the test set. Early stopping triggered at epoch 6 (patience = 3), with the best checkpoint saved at epoch 3 (validation macro-F1 = 0.5714).

**Classification Report Summary:**
* **Ham (Legitimate):** 71% Precision / 100% Recall / F1 = 0.83
* **Phish (Malicious):** 67% Precision / 100% Recall / F1 = 0.80
* **Spam (Commercial Junk):** 0% Precision / 0% Recall / F1 = 0.00

**Confusion Matrix:**
| True Label \ Predicted | Predicted Ham | Predicted Phish | Predicted Spam |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **True Ham** | 5 | 0 | 0 |
| **True Phish** | 0 | 6 | 0 |
| **True Spam** | **2** | **3** | 0 |

---

## 2. What Do These Metrics Actually Mean?

* **Strong on Ham and Phish:** The model correctly identified all 5 Ham and all 6 Phish emails (100% recall for both). It can reliably distinguish legitimate communication from phishing attempts.
* **Complete Spam Blind Spot:** The model failed to classify any Spam email correctly. All 5 Spam samples were misclassified — 2 as Ham and 3 as Phish. The model essentially learned a 2-class boundary (Ham vs. Phish) and ignored Spam entirely.
* **Compared to Traditional Baseline:** The TF-IDF + SVM baseline outperformed the neural baseline on this dataset (83% accuracy vs. 68.75%). The SVM correctly classified at least some Spam messages, while DistilBERT classified none.

---

## 3. Why Did the Neural Baseline Underperform?

The poor performance is expected and can be attributed to two key factors:

**Small Dataset Size:**
With only 105 training samples (36 Spam, 40 Phish, 29 Ham), DistilBERT has far too few examples to fine-tune its 66M parameters effectively. Transformer models are powerful, but they are data-hungry — they typically need thousands of labelled examples to learn reliable decision boundaries during fine-tuning.

**Catastrophic Forgetting on Minority Patterns:**
During fine-tuning, gradient updates from the more frequent (and perhaps more distinctive) Phish and Ham classes dominate. The model converges on a shortcut: defaulting to a Ham-or-Phish prediction for everything. Despite class-weighted loss, 36 Spam samples are simply not enough for the model to learn what makes Spam distinct from the other two classes at the contextual level.

---

## 4. What the Neural Approach Still Offers

Despite the low headlining numbers, the DistilBERT experiment is not wasted effort. It reveals something important:

* **Contextual embeddings capture semantic similarity.** The fact that Spam was split between Ham and Phish (rather than collapsed into one) shows the model is picking up on real semantic overlap — Spam shares conversational tone with Ham and urgency/action language with Phish.
* **Pretrained representations are still valuable.** Even without successful end-to-end fine-tuning, DistilBERT's hidden-layer embeddings encode rich contextual features that could be extracted and used as input to a simpler classifier.

---

## Conclusion & Next Steps

For our **final submission baseline**, we will use the **traditional method (TF-IDF + SVM)** as our primary baseline, because it performs substantially better on our current small dataset (83% accuracy vs. 68.75%, with actual Spam recall).

However, the neural approach remains valuable as a **feature extraction tool**. In future sprints, we plan to:

1. **Use DistilBERT as a feature extractor** — extract `[CLS]` token embeddings from the pretrained model (without fine-tuning) and feed them into a traditional classifier (e.g., SVM, Logistic Regression). This combines the contextual understanding of transformers with the data-efficiency of traditional methods.
2. **Augment training data** — use paraphrasing or back-translation to synthetically expand the Spam class before fine-tuning.
3. **Explore hybrid architectures** — concatenate TF-IDF features with neural embeddings to capture both surface-level lexical patterns and deeper semantic features.

The core insight is: **traditional methods win on small data, but neural representations capture semantic nuances that TF-IDF cannot.** Our final system should leverage both strengths.
